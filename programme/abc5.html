Training Programme

AI, Blockchain &
Cybersecurity

ABC5

ABC5 is a cross-domain training programme for people who need to understand how modern digital systems are built, trusted, and defended.

Instead of treating AI, blockchain, and cybersecurity as separate silos, ABC5 helps participants understand how they interact in practice — across products, policy, infrastructure, and governance.

About the Programme

Why ABC5 exists

Technology work rarely stays in one domain. A team deploying AI still needs to think about security, identity, data integrity, and system trust. A policymaker working on digital governance cannot make good decisions with only surface-level technical understanding.

ABC5 was created for that reality. It gives participants a structured foundation across three fields that increasingly shape the same systems, decisions, and risks.

What ABC5 Covers

Three domains, one programme

ABC5 is built across three connected areas. Each has its own concepts and tools, but the programme is designed to help participants see how they overlap rather than treating them as unrelated subjects.

Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence

Participants explore how AI systems are developed, deployed, and governed — from data and models to tools, agents, and accountability. The goal is not only to understand what AI can do, but how to ask what a system is actually doing and what responsibilities come with using it.

Blockchain & Trust Technologies

Blockchain & Trust Technologies

This part of the programme covers cryptography fundamentals, consensus, smart contracts, digital identity, and tokenisation. ABC5 approaches blockchain as a family of trust technologies, not as a narrow conversation about speculation or markets.

Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity

Participants learn to think in terms of threat models, attack surface, secure design, and operational risk. Cybersecurity is treated as something built in from the start, not added at the end after a system already exists.

What Makes It Distinctive

The intersection matters

What makes ABC5 different is not simply that it includes three topics. It is that it teaches the connections between them.

Participants are encouraged to think across boundaries: how AI systems create new security risks, how trust infrastructure shapes digital identity, how governance decisions depend on technical realities, and how weak understanding in one area creates blind spots in another.

Who It Is For

Who ABC5 is for

ABC5 is designed for serious learners who need cross-domain understanding, even if they begin from very different backgrounds.

University students

For those building foundational literacy before entering research, industry, or public-facing technology work.

Technical professionals

For those already strong in one area who need a clearer grasp of the others as their work becomes more interconnected.

Policy and compliance practitioners

For those responsible for systems they may not have built, but still need to evaluate, govern, or regulate well.

Educators and trainers

For those teaching emerging technology and looking for a better integrated framework than isolated topic-by-topic delivery.

What Participants Gain

What you take away

ABC5 is designed to leave participants with more than vocabulary. By the end of the programme, they should have a clearer mental model of how these domains fit together and where the key risks, opportunities, and decision points sit.

The programme is especially valuable for people who are tired of fragmented learning and want a more coherent map of the emerging-technology landscape.

Relationship to Certification

Training first, certification separately

ABC5 is a training programme. It builds cross-domain literacy and applied understanding, but it is not itself a formal certification.

Formal credentials are issued separately through ISFRETIC, KBI’s independent certification framework. ABC5 may provide relevant preparation, but competence is demonstrated through assessment rather than attendance.

Delivery

How cohorts are run

ABC5 cohorts are typically opened when the right institutional or community partner is in place. This allows the format, depth, and examples used in the programme to be adjusted to the audience, rather than forcing every cohort into the same template.

Some editions may be more student-focused. Others may be designed for professionals, educators, or partner organisations exploring a specific need.
